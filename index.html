The plan

Four phases. Each one is concrete. Each step is a thing you do, not a thing you consider.

Phase one · this month

Fix the 401(k) you already have

The single biggest leak in your current setup is the fee on your fund. You are in the R‑4 share class at 0.71%. The same fund exists in cheaper share classes — usually R‑6 — at roughly a third of that. Over fourteen years, that one switch is worth tens of thousands of dollars in compounded growth.

  1. Log into your 401(k) plan portal. Find the full fund menu.
  2. Look for one of these, ordered by preference:
    • An index target‑date fund: Vanguard Target Retirement 2040, Fidelity Freedom Index 2040, or Schwab Target Index 2040 — fees of 0.08% to 0.12%.
    • If no index TDF: the R‑6 share class of American Funds 2040 (ticker RFETX) — fees around 0.33%.
    • If neither is offered: any S&P 500 or total‑US‑stock index fund the plan has, paired with a bond index fund.
  3. Switch your existing balance and all future contributions to the cheaper fund. Keep the 2040 retirement‑year vintage — it matches your timeline.
  4. Increase your contribution rate from whatever it is now to at minimum 15% of salary — that is $17,850 a year. If you can afford it, the maximum is $32,500 a year in 2026 ($24,500 base plus an $8,000 catch‑up because you are over 50).
  5. Confirm your employer match is being fully captured. Whatever percentage they match to, contribute at least that much. The match is free money. Missing it is the only mistake that costs more than picking individual stocks.

Phase two · next three months

Build the parallel structure

One account is not a plan, it is a single point of failure. You need three streams running in parallel: the 401(k) for pre‑tax growth, a taxable brokerage for flexible compounding, and an HSA if you have access to one. Each does something the others cannot.

  1. Open a taxable brokerage account at Fidelity or Vanguard. It is free and takes fifteen minutes online. No advisor required.
  2. Set up an automatic deposit from each paycheck — 10% of gross, every cycle, before the money touches your checking account. On a $119K salary that is roughly $991 a month. Treat it like rent you pay to your future self.
  3. Inside that brokerage, buy three funds and nothing else:
    • VTI — total US stock market — 70% of every deposit
    • VXUS — total international stock market — 20%
    • BND — total US bond market — 10%
  4. If you have access to an HSA through work, max it. In 2026 that is $4,400 self‑only or $8,750 family. Do not leave it sitting in cash — invest it in the same three funds above. It is the most tax‑advantaged account that exists in American law.
  5. Open a Roth IRA at the same brokerage. Contribute the maximum: $7,500 plus $1,100 catch‑up for being over 50, totaling $8,600 a year. If your income is over the Roth contribution limit you will need to do the "backdoor" — search "backdoor Roth IRA Fidelity step by step" for the five‑step process.
  6. Build an emergency fund. Six to twelve months of your actual expenses, parked in a high‑yield savings account earning around 4%. Marcus by Goldman Sachs, Ally, or Wealthfront Cash. This is the layer that keeps you from selling your investments during a bad month.

Phase three · within twelve months

Protect what you are building

You do not have a partner, family next door, or a community network to catch you if your health turns. That is the single biggest unmodeled risk to everything above. The mathematics of compounding does not care about a three‑year nursing home stay; it just goes to zero. Insurance is how the math survives a body.

  1. Get long‑term care insurance quotes this year. You are 51. Premiums roughly double after age 55, and underwriting gets stricter every year you wait. Request quotes from Mutual of Omaha, Northwestern Mutual, and New York Life. Also ask about hybrid life/LTC policies, which behave more like an asset than a sunk premium.
  2. Confirm you have disability insurance through your employer. If your coverage is less than 60% of salary or excludes long‑term disability, buy an individual policy. With no spouse, there is no second income to fall back on.
  3. Spend $1,500 to $3,000 on an estate attorney, once. You will get:
    • A will naming specific beneficiaries so your state's intestacy law does not decide for you.
    • A healthcare power of attorney — name someone you trust, or if none exists, a licensed professional fiduciary in your state.
    • A living will (advance directive) covering end‑of‑life medical decisions.
    • Updated beneficiary designations on every account. These override the will — get them right.
  4. If you have pets, add pet‑care provisions to your will: who takes them, with what funds, under what conditions.

Phase four · year one and ongoing

Tax‑optimize the whole thing

A small amount of professional help, once, locks in choices that compound for the next thirty years. The trick is buying it the right way — flat fees, not a percentage of your assets. The percentage model quietly eats six figures.

  1. Pay for one consultation with a fee‑only fiduciary CFP. Flat fee, $1,500 to $3,000, one time. You can find one at NAPFA.org or XYPlanningNetwork.com. Specifically ask them to decide:
    • Roth vs Traditional 401(k) split, based on your expected retirement bracket
    • Social Security claiming age — 62, 67, or 70 (the difference is over $200K in lifetime benefits)
    • Asset location: which account holds which type of investment
  2. Find a CPA who does tax planning, not just tax filing. $500 to $1,000 a year. Ask them about:
    • Tax‑loss harvesting in your taxable brokerage
    • Whether your 401(k) plan offers after‑tax contributions plus in‑service conversions — the "mega backdoor Roth," which could add up to $46,000 a year of extra Roth space
    • State‑tax optimization if you ever move in retirement
  3. Apply the asset‑location rule: keep bonds inside your 401(k) where ordinary‑income tax is deferred, keep equity index funds in the taxable brokerage where long‑term capital gains are taxed at lower rates. Most investors get this backwards and pay a hidden penalty for it.

The rules

Write these once, while you are calm. Re‑read them when you are not.

— A pledge to yourself —

  1. I will not check the balance more than once a month.
  2. I will not sell during a market drop. Drops are normal. I expect them.
  3. I will not chase a hot stock, a cryptocurrency, or whatever is in the news.
  4. I will not take a 401(k) loan, for any reason short of true emergency.
  5. I will not move my money to an advisor who charges a percentage of assets. Fee‑only or nothing.
  6. I will rebalance once a year, in January, and otherwise leave it alone.
  7. I will increase my contribution percentage every year that I get a raise.
  8. I will re‑read this document every January 1st, and update it for changes in tax law.

When things change

A short list of moments the rest of the plan is silent about, and what the correct answer is anyway.

If the market drops 30%.
Do not sell. Keep contributing. A 30% drop is the price index‑fund investors pay roughly once a decade for the long‑run return. The contributions you make during the drop are the ones that compound the hardest. The worst investors are the ones who panic; the second‑worst are the ones who stop contributing until things "settle."
If you change jobs.
Roll the old 401(k) into a rollover IRA at Fidelity or Vanguard. Do not cash it out. Do not leave it at the old employer's plan unless it has unusually cheap institutional funds. The IRA gives you control of share class and gives you room for future Roth conversions.
If you get a raise.
Increase your 401(k) contribution percentage first, then enjoy what is left. Lifestyle creep is the single biggest reason mid‑career professionals retire poor. The raise is the easiest moment to redirect, because you never got used to having the money.
If you inherit money or get a windfall.
Do nothing for six months. Park it in the high‑yield savings account. Make no decisions in grief, excitement, or under pressure from anyone who suddenly has opinions about your finances. After six months, follow the plan above: max accounts in order, fill the emergency fund, then the taxable brokerage.
If someone offers to manage your money.
Ask them, in writing, two questions. Are you a fiduciary, all the time, to me, in writing? And, do you charge a flat fee or a percentage of my assets? If the answer to the first is no, walk. If the answer to the second is "percentage," walk. There are no exceptions.

What you are deliberately not doing

Every item on this list is something most people lose money on. Knowing why you are skipping them is part of the discipline.

  • Individual stock picking. Across any twenty‑year window, roughly 95% of stock‑pickers underperform a plain index fund. The 5% who win are mostly luck. You don't need to win — you need to not lose.
  • Market timing. The biggest single‑day gains historically happen within days of the biggest single‑day drops. Selling locks you out of the recovery. Holding through both is the entire game.
  • Crypto as an allocation. No income stream, no proven inflation hedge across a real cycle, extreme volatility. If you want exposure for fun, cap it at 1–2% of net worth and treat the rest as zero. Do not put retirement money there.
  • Annuities. Sold hard for a reason — the commissions are enormous. They lock your money up, charge high internal fees, and rarely beat what you'd earn doing the boring index plan above.
  • "Alternative investments" pitched in person. Private real estate funds, structured notes, life‑settlement contracts, art funds. If someone in a suit is explaining it to you, it is almost certainly designed to pay them, not you.
  • An advisor on a percentage‑of‑assets fee. A 1% AUM fee on $1 million is $10,000 every year, forever. Over thirty years that compounds to more than a quarter of your portfolio. Flat fees only.
